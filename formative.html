The Fedora: A contemporary Analysis

Introduction

These days the fedora is infamous. It was once the height of fashion, the pinnacle of style, a symbol of grace. Modern times have not been kind to this garment, but I believe it is soon due a rebirth. With a rich past and promising future, the fedora may soon become the staple of 21st century style.

What is a fedora?

A fedora is a hat with a soft brim and indented crown. It is typically creased lengthwise down the crown and "pinched" near the front on both sides. Fedoras can also be creased with teardrop crowns, diamond crowns, center dents, and others, and the positioning of pinches can vary. The typical crown height is 4.5 inches (11 cm). The term fedora was in use as early as 1891. Its popularity soared, and eventually it eclipsed the similar-looking homburg.

A History

The word fedora was first used as the title of an 1882 play by Victorein Sardou. Princess Fedora Romanov, the heroine of the play, wore a center-creased, soft brimmed hat. It was adopted by the women's rights movement as a symbol. After Edward, Prince of Wales started to wear them in 1924, it became popular among men for its stylishness and weather protection. During the Prohibition era of the United States, fedoras were associated with gangsters to create a 'tough guy' image. Indiana Jones re-popularized the fedora in the 1980s.

Revival

On February 17th, 2009, the men's lifestyle blog Details published an article titled "Cool or Tool?: The Fedora," which urged readers to carefully consider incorporating a fedora into their outfits. On July 20th, Urban Dictionary user Ricky Torro submitted an entry for the word "fedorka," defining the term as a pejorative for a person attempting to be hip or trendy by wearing the fashion accessory.

Mockery

The shameful mockery of the fedora was initiated in the early 2010s, as it became what is known as a 'meme' across various online social media websites. Just as quickly as this garment has fallen into disrepute, I am convinced it can once again rise to the highest peaks of modern style.
